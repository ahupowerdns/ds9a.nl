Startup & Innovation Library

I care a lot about innovation. Given today's industrial architecture, that means I care a lot about startups, since large corporations are now mostly process oriented, leaving actual innovation to smaller organizations.

Partially on request of Yes!Delft, the TU Delft startup incubator, I have created this 'Library' page with links to various articles relevant to innovation & startup life. I also include some of my own work among the links.

I know very little about the 'business to consumer' business, so if you are looking for how to create the new Twitter or the new AirBNB, this page is thin on advice. But there is a lot about innovation and business to business.

Articles and blogs:
  • Camels and Rubber Duckies - on pricing. Joel Spolsky has a lot more relevant things to say too.
  • Marc Andreessen's startup compendium. Of specific note, The Moby Dick theory of big companies and Why the initial business plan does not matter that much
  • Mark Suster's blog. Today he is a VC, but he used to be an entrepreneur. Now he shares his own pearls of wisdom
  • Looking bigger, on how to solve the problem of any startup: no serious enterprise wants to do business with a tiny company.
  • Meet the new enterprise customer, he's a lot like the old customer, on how large organizations buy things, and why you will probably need serious (and expensive) sales guys
  • The Schlep - your startup WILL involve some really hard work that is entirely unsexy. There's no avoiding it. Seen another way, it is why other people aren't where you want to be yet. They haven't done.. the schlep.

Books:
  • The Innovator's Dilemma - When New Technologies Cause Great Firms to Fail, relevant when you want to compete with established firms, explains why you have a very good chance. Contrast with the 'Moby Dick theory' above.
  • Good Strategy, Bad Strategy, 37 years in the making, a very tight book on what strategy is and how to leverage your strenghts. Good review by John Kay
  • The Founder's Dilemmas, very thorough investigation of (un)successful startups, discusses most relevant aspects
